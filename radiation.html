#21
2.5MeV photon undergoes a 120 degree Compton scattering. How much energy does the scattered electron possess?
  • Energy of a scattered photon after Compton scattering is given by: 
  • \(E' = \frac{E_0}{1 + \frac{E_0}{0.511} (1 - \cos \Phi)}\)
  • Thus, \(E' = \frac{2.5}{1 + \frac{2.5}{0.511}(1 - \cos 120^\circ)} = 0.3 \,\text{MeV}\)
  • Because of conservation of momentum we know that \(E_0 = E' + E_{\text{electron}}, \quad \text{so} \quad E_{\text{electron}} = E_0 - E' = 2.5 - 0.3 = 2.2 \,\text{MeV}\)

A 6MeV photon back-scatters off of an electron. What fraction of its original energy does the photon retain after this scattering event?
  • \(\text{Compton formula: } E' = \frac{E_0}{1 + \frac{E_0}{0.511} (1 - \cos \Phi)}\)
  • \(\text{Backscatter: } \Phi = 180^\circ, \cos 180^\circ = -1 \implies E' = \frac{6}{1 + \frac{6}{0.511} \cdot 2}\)
  • \(\frac{6}{0.511} \approx 11.74, \quad 1 + 11.74 \cdot 2 = 24.48\)
  • \(E' = \frac{6}{24.48} \approx 0.245\ \text{MeV}\)
  • \(\text{Fraction of original energy retained: } \frac{E'}{E_0} = \frac{0.245}{6} \approx 0.041 \ (\text{or } 4.1\%)\)

  • It is equal to 0.876 for air in a Co-60 beam.

  • f-factor is used to convert exposure (X) to absorbed dose (D) in a particular material
  • f = D/X
  • D = absorbed dose in the material (Gy)
  • X = exposure (C/kg) measured in air
  • f accounts for differences in energy absorption between air and the material of interest.

  • 1. “It is generally higher for high-Z materials.”

    • True. High atomic number materials absorb more energy per unit exposure, so the f-factor increases with Z. ✅

  • 2. “It is equal to 0.971 for water in a 1 MeV beam.”

    • True. This is a standard value from dosimetry tables. ✅

  • 3. “It is generally lower for low energy beams.”

    • False. For low-energy photons, the f-factor actually increases, especially in water, because the mass energy absorption coefficient is higher at low energies. 
    • At low photon energies (tens to hundreds of keV):
      • Photoelectric effect dominates in materials like water.
        • Photoelectric absorption scales strongly with atomic number ZZZ: higher Z → more absorption.

  • 4. “It is equal to 0.876 for air in a Co-60 beam.”

    • True. This is a standard Co-60 value for air. ✅
#31
A novel radiation source has a half life of 2 days. By about what percent does the source decay in 1 hour?
  • \(N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \quad \text{(exponential decay law)}\)
  • \(N(T_{1/2}) = \frac{N_0}{2} = N_0 e^{-\lambda T_{1/2}} \implies \lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{T_{1/2}} \quad \text{(relate decay constant to half-life)}\)
  • \(\text{Fraction remaining after } \Delta t: \frac{N(\Delta t)}{N_0} = e^{-\lambda \Delta t} = e^{-(\ln 2 / T_{1/2}) \Delta t} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\Delta t / T_{1/2}}\)
  • \(\text{Fraction decayed: } \text{Fraction decayed} = 1 - \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\Delta t / T_{1/2}}\)
  • \(\text{Example: } \Delta t = 1\ \text{hour, } T_{1/2} = 48\ \text{hours} \implies 1 - (0.5)^{1/48} \approx 0.014 = 1.4\%\)

#40
An I-125 seed (half life = 59.4 days) delivers an initial dose rate of 2.4cGy/hr to its reference point. What is the cumulative dose delivered after  30 days?

  • \(\dot{D}(t) = \dot{D}_0 e^{-\lambda t} \quad \text{(dose rate decreases exponentially)}\)
  • \(\lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{T_{1/2}} = \frac{0.693}{59.4\ \text{days}} \approx 0.01166\ \text{day}^{-1} \quad \text{(decay constant)}\)
  • \(\dot{D}_t = \dot{D}_0 e^{-\lambda t} = 2.4 \cdot e^{-0.01166 \cdot 30} \approx 1.69\ \text{cGy/hr} \quad \text{(dose rate at t = 30 days)}\)
  • \(T_\text{mean} = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{0.01166} \approx 85.7\ \text{days} \approx 2057\ \text{hours} \quad \text{(mean life of the source)}\)
  • \(D_\text{cum} \approx (\dot{D}_0 - \dot{D}_t) \cdot T_\text{mean} \quad \text{(average dose-rate approach)}\)
  • \(D_\text{cum} \approx (2.4 - 1.69) \cdot 2057 \approx 1460\ \text{cGy} \approx 14.6\ \text{Gy} \quad \text{(cumulative dose after 30 days)}\)

Think of it this way. Neutrons exist as a way to stabilize all the + charge in the nucleus. Decay happens when the nucleus of an atom is not stable either because it has too many neutrons, or not enough neutrons. (Or its too big, which is where alpha decay likely comes in)
Too many neutrons: wants to get rid of neutrons. This can be done by Beta minus decay. Existing neutron turns into proton, electron 

Too many protons: wants to GAIN neutrons. There’s two ways that atoms do this. Beta plus decay (AKA positron emission) where a proton turns into a neutron, positron. It’s been found that there’s another way atoms decay to solve this problem, which is through electron capture. EC works by the proton literally capturing an electron from its own shell and turning that into a neutron. Think of it as like opposite charges clashing to make a neutrally charged neutron. Both work to fix the same problem of having too many protons, or in other words not enough neutrons. Radioisotopes that have too many protons/not enough neutrons will have a certain percentage of how much of each substance uses EC vs positron emission, this percentage is characteristic for each radioisotope.

#86
What is the key modification to BG theory introduced by the Spencer-Attix formulation?
  • Assumes all secondary electrons are deposited locally
  • Removal of the requirement for CPE
  • Works well for large cavities
  • Introduction of a cutoff energy
Introduction of a cutoff energy
  • Bragg-Gray (BG) theory:
    • Relates dose in a small cavity to dose in the surrounding medium
    • Assumes charged particle equilibrium (CPE)
    • Assumes all secondary electrons deposit energy locally
  • Problem with original BG theory:

  • Spencer-Attix modification:
    • Introduces a cutoff energy (Δ)
      • Only electrons above Δ are considered “non-local” and handled explicitly
      • Electrons below Δ are assumed to deposit energy locally
    • Improves accuracy for electron and photon dosimetry
